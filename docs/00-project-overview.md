# Project Society — Project Overview

**Status:** Approved baseline  
**Audience:** Project owner, designers, architects, developers and Codex

## 1. Product Statement

Project Society is a persistent, browser-based society simulator in which players enter a living world and choose their own purpose.

There is no campaign to complete, universal character level to reach or final victory condition. A player may become a skilled farmer, employee, industrialist, property developer, banker, teacher, researcher, mayor, police officer, organiser, criminal, soldier or any combination that the world's rules permit.

The world is a shared simulation rather than a collection of isolated matches. It continues to change when individual players are offline. Economic activity, production, crops, loans, governments, NPC lives and infrastructure continue according to their own rules.

The central promise is:

> The world exists with or without the player. The player chooses a place within it and can leave a lasting mark.

## 2. Product Identity

Project Society combines characteristics of several genres without becoming subordinate to one of them:

- persistent online world
- economic and industrial simulation
- social sandbox
- political simulation
- property and infrastructure simulation
- skill-based character progression
- time-based browser game
- strategy game with plot-based conflict

It is not intended to be:

- a graphical first-person or third-person open-world game
- a twitch combat game
- a conventional quest-driven MMORPG
- a spreadsheet that requires accounting expertise
- an idle game that plays itself without meaningful player choices
- a full scientific simulation of every living organism or physical process

## 3. Player Fantasy

The player's fantasy is not necessarily power. It is **participation, mastery and consequence**.

Examples of valid self-created ambitions include:

- produce the world's highest-quality grain
- become the most trusted builder in a region
- work a stable job and own a comfortable home
- create a vertically integrated vehicle company
- discover an important mineral deposit and sell the survey rights
- build and rent a portfolio of apartment buildings
- establish a university known for energy research
- win an election and transform local policy
- organise a labour union or religious community
- build a criminal network and evade investigation
- operate transport links that connect distant markets
- serve as a highly experienced police investigator

The simulator must not favour entrepreneurship so strongly that being an employee becomes invalid. A player who prefers predictable work should have a meaningful, sustainable experience.

## 4. Core Experience Pillars

### 4.1 A persistent world

The world is never reset as a normal operating mechanic. It may be updated, repaired and expanded, but history, ownership and social development are intended to persist for many years.

### 4.2 A player-driven society

Players can own property, work, trade, form companies, establish organisations, pass laws, operate public services and commit crimes. NPCs ensure continuity, especially early in the world's life, but players are the preferred source of excellence, ambition and disruption.

### 4.3 Connected systems

Systems must interact rather than operate as isolated minigames.

A farmer depends on soil, weather, equipment, energy, transport, markets and labour. A vehicle depends on mined resources, processed materials, components, factories, logistics, research and sales. A municipality depends on residents, taxes, services, infrastructure and policy.

### 4.4 Meaningful time

Actions take time. Movement, work, education, research, construction, production, investigation and crime may continue while the player is offline. Time creates planning and trade-offs, but should not force constant attendance at the screen.

### 4.5 Mastery through specific experience

A character gains experience in the work actually performed. Mining experience improves mining; farming experience improves farming; teaching experience improves teaching. Education can accelerate development and unlock qualified roles, but it does not replace practical experience.

### 4.6 Quality and reputation

Quality is created by the entire chain: source materials, worker skill, tools, facilities, process and research. Better goods can perform better, last longer and build reputations for the people and organisations that produced them.

### 4.7 Limited and earned information

The map is not an omniscient database. Presence, exploration, surveys, ownership, employees, friendship, public records and technology determine what a character can know.

## 5. Primary Interaction Model

The main experience is a hybrid between a map interface and contextual management panels.

The player normally sees:

- a two-dimensional world map divided into plots
- terrain, settlements, public infrastructure and known map information
- the player's location and permitted shared friend locations
- contextual actions for the current plot
- panels for inventory, work, property, companies, organisations and government
- an in-world phone or computer interface for remote services

Clicking a remote plot does not reveal all live information. A player may need to travel there, possess a recent observation, employ someone there, own sensors or consult an authorised registry.

Physical work generally requires the character to be present. Administrative decisions may be made remotely when the character has suitable communication technology and authority.

## 6. Fundamental Player Loop

A common loop is:

1. inspect needs, opportunities and current location
2. choose a goal or action
3. obtain required access, tools, materials or transport
4. travel when physical presence is required
5. start an authoritative timed action
6. leave the game or perform permitted parallel administration
7. return after completion
8. receive output, XP, information and consequences
9. trade, consume, invest, improve or choose the next action

The loop applies to work, production, education, research, construction, travel and many crimes. The exact duration depends on distance, infrastructure, skill, equipment, condition and other domain rules.

## 7. Accessibility and Complexity

The simulator should be deep internally and understandable externally.

Players decide:

- what to produce
- where to build
- whom to employ
- what price or wage to offer
- whether to borrow or invest
- which policies to support
- what risks to accept

The system handles routine mechanics such as:

- accounting entries
- payroll deductions
- tax collection
- interest accrual
- basic utility billing
- recipe arithmetic
- routine NPC execution

Complexity is justified when it creates a meaningful decision, visible consequence or useful specialisation. Complexity that only creates clerical work should be automated or abstracted.

## 8. World Scale and Growth

The world consists of one planet with continents, regions, oceans and plots.

The planet may be generated with more land than is initially accessible. Selected continents can be opened at launch while others remain unavailable or primarily NPC-operated. New regions or continents may open when population, economic density or technical capacity justifies expansion.

Expansion must feel like opening an existing part of the same world, not creating a disconnected new server or resetting history.

The exact plot dimensions and world capacity remain open balance and technical decisions. The data model must avoid requiring every theoretical plot to be fully simulated every second.

## 9. Technology Level

The world begins at a broadly modern technological level. Vehicles, electricity, computers, internet services, banking, medicine and industrial production are conceptually available, provided the required organisations, knowledge, infrastructure and supply chains exist.

Research does not begin from the stone age. It improves methods, unlocks specialised capabilities, reduces waste, changes recipes, improves quality and creates new variants.

Advanced and dangerous infrastructure, such as nuclear power, requires suitable research, qualified workers, facilities, regulation and capital.

## 10. Social Structure

Four major legal and social actor types exist:

1. **Characters** — player or NPC persons with location, needs, skills, property and relationships.
2. **Companies** — profit-seeking legal entities with ownership, assets, staff and obligations.
3. **Governments** — public authorities at village, regional or national levels.
4. **Organisations** — member-based groups such as political parties, religions, unions, clubs, charities and interest groups.

These actor types may own assets and enter permitted transactions, but their governance, purpose and powers differ.

## 11. Economic Position

The economy uses one fictional base currency at launch. The currency name remains an open naming decision.

The economy supports scarcity, supply and demand, credit, interest, collateral, default, bankruptcy, taxes, wages, investment and property speculation. Economic failure is allowed. A company, bank or municipality may make poor choices and face real consequences.

Players may not buy the world currency or economic advantages for real money. The future funding model for operating the game is outside this baseline, but it must not compromise the simulated economy or create pay-to-win power.

## 12. Consequence and Safety

The simulation is intentionally capable of loss:

- businesses may fail
- loans may default
- property may be repossessed
- crops may underperform
- goods may wear out
- crimes may lead to arrest and imprisonment
- characters may be injured or killed
- regions may decline

A killed character returns through a defined recovery or respawn process rather than losing the entire account, all skills and all long-term ownership. Companies should normally be owned through legal entities so they can survive a character's temporary death.

New players receive explanation and limited onboarding protection, not permanent immunity from the world.

## 13. Public API

Project Society is intended to expose a controlled API.

Permitted uses may include:

- personal statistics and dashboards
- price analysis
- map and survey tools using information the character lawfully knows
- notifications
- organisation integrations
- AI-assisted or scripted character control within documented limits

The API is not a second, privileged game client. Every command must obey the same costs, timers, presence requirements, permissions and rate limits as an official interface action.

## 14. Scope Discipline

The long-term vision is large, but the implementation must grow through coherent vertical slices.

A feature should be added when it:

- creates new meaningful choices
- connects to existing systems
- can be simulated safely and understandably
- has a credible path to testing and balancing
- does not require unsustainable continuous computation

The first playable release should prove that a small region can live: characters can move, work, produce, trade, own property and interact with an economy that continues while they are offline.

## 15. Product Success

Project Society succeeds when players can tell stories that were not scripted by the developers.

Examples include:

- a town becoming prosperous because a transport company connected it to a mine
- a famous farm brand emerging from unusually good soil and skilled workers
- a bank collapsing after aggressive property lending
- a political movement changing local laws
- a polluted fishing area recovering after regulation and industrial upgrades
- a criminal organisation exploiting a shortage and being investigated by player police

The measure is not merely feature count. It is whether the shared systems produce understandable, consequential and socially interesting outcomes.
